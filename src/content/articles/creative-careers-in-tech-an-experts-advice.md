<blockquote id="">Tags: creative career, tech career, creative tech career, how to get into tech, how to be a creative, how to have creative career, day in the life of a designer, be a designer, get into design, get into UX, get into UI, jobs for creative people, jobs for divergent thinkers, jobs for ADHD, jobs for neurodivergent, neurodiverse jobs, ADHD jobs, career paths based on personal qualities</blockquote><p id=""></p><p id="">I was asked to write this article by Career Foundry, where I was a UX Tutor and sat on the Instructor Advisory Board.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Creative tech careers require a combination of creative skills and technological understanding, but also something special:<strong id=""><em id=""> </em></strong>learned thought processes, like divergent and critical thinking.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">It’s a myth that you need to be born with artistic talent to be good at design. Good functional (UX) or visual (UI) design is often created by people who can’t draw.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Traditional education doesn’t value creativity much, and likes to tell us that there is only one right answer to every question. But a person with a good imagination can come up with loads of answers when asked “What is a paper clip used for?”&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Schools try to erase our imagination &amp; creativity, but these are exactly the skills that are so valuable in creative tech careers.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">It is possible to unlearn the constrained approach of traditional education and become more creative by learning new ways of thinking.</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">How I got started in design</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">My parents told me that if I pursued an art degree, I’d end up working at a supermarket till.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">How wrong they were. Today, creative careers are amongst the best paid and most enjoyable. I also believe that this trend will continue, a theory well explained in Ken Robinson’s book “The Element: How Finding Your Passion Changes Everything.” (I highly recommend this book and Ken’s YouTube videos if you’re starting your creative career journey.)</p><p id=""></p><p id="">I graduated in Product Design 1996. My degree focused on physical products, which sometimes featured small digital screens running simple software.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">In 2024 that’s completely reversed: now the majority of the products being designed are software, like apps on phones.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">For many people of my generation, I had a very typical design career, from visual design through to web design and then to UX design.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Between graduating and working in visual design, I worked as a C programmer—that’s not so typical. I also found that I really didn’t like coding. However, I was pretty good at information architecture, i.e. designing what the software would do.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">My personal experience has been very mixed: I love designing daily and feel that I found the right career early on, despite an attempted side-track by my parents.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">I love the work itself, and I’ve worked at some really fun advertising agencies with amazing offices surrounded by smart creative people.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">A day in the life of a designer</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">I now work with multiple income streams between product design clients, tutoring for CareerFoundry, private online design sessions, and building up educational materials for Artfuly.com.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Here’s an example of how creativity played into my role as a designer for <a id="" href="http://terralistens.com" target="_blank">Terra</a>, a nature sounds app.</p><p id=""></p><figure id="" class="w-richtext-figure-type-image w-richtext-align-fullwidth" style="max-width:967px" data-rt-type="image" data-rt-align="fullwidth" data-rt-max-width="967px"><div id=""><img id="" alt="" src="/images/webflow-cdn/660176ac6c67801d69424235_Rdqov5WoCPQrSza7BKkURYO624ccl0cHVq9OJ484yu1_1ISWF3293T7Y1F8cxbhy3T80bELCOBExh99RjuT-Oh45BOdcR2ZCF15FVHEUWOIh8QoeCgCAdoGXT2FcbFgBRGIXonTOzl8J8saa1Hg3wLo.png" width="auto" height="auto" loading="auto"></div></figure><p id=""></p><p id="">We were having issues with the live sound feeds breaking, so I created a design to allow pre-recorded sound to be playable if the live stream went offline. This replaced the “Under maintenance” message, which I felt wasn’t a good user experience.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">I then designed the updated interface to include the pre-recorded sound. I wrote the annotations: for example, if a user has set a wake up sound, and the live stream becomes unavailable, then the pre-recorded sound would auto-play instead.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Then I briefed the developer and reviewed the work once it’s completed. That’s just one example of product development and problem solving necessary to bring concepts to life.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Besides working for clients as a product designer, I also teach design students online, and write design courses.</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">How creativity and scientific processes meet&nbsp;</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">In recent years, a trend has grown: design that emphasizes aesthetics and superficial visual appeal over functionality or real world usability.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">I call this the “Dribbbleisation” of design, after the design network Dribbble.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Don’t mistake this for real design.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id=""><strong id="">Real design combines creativity with a rather scientific approach.</strong></p><p id=""></p><p id="">Let’s take a deeper look at some of the steps involved in UX design.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">A UX designer goes through three main steps to create a new product concept:&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><ol id=""><li id=""><strong id="">User research</strong>, asking the target users what they value</li><li id=""><strong id="">Prototyping a concept </strong>based on that research</li><li id=""><strong id="">User testing</strong> to validate and find issues to fix.</li></ol><p id=""></p><p id="">This is an oversimplification, but it’s a scientific thought process more than it is an artistic one.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">The real “magic sauce” that sets apart a great creative is the ability to create a <strong id="">concept </strong>based on the research, utilizing divergent and critical thinking.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">What is divergent thinking?</strong></h3><p id=""></p><p id=""><em id="">Divergent thinking</em> involves generating multiple creative solutions or ideas for a problem. It emphasizes exploring various possibilities, perspectives, and unconventional approaches to find innovative solutions.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Unlike<em id=""> convergent thinking</em>, which narrows down options to find the best solution, divergent thinking encourages broad exploration and openness to new and original ideas.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Watch this video about divergent width="auto" height="auto" loading="auto"></div></a></figure><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">Which skills are needed for a creative career in tech?</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">Successful tech creatives have a combination of technical skills, creativity, and soft skills like:</p><ul id=""><li id=""><strong id="">Problem solving</strong>: Identify challenges and develop creative solutions.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Technical skills</strong>: Proficiency in design software, design principles, and relevant tools and techniques.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Creativity</strong>: A strong sense of innovation to generate unique, functional and visually appealing designs.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Communication</strong>: Effectively convey ideas, collaborate with team members, and understand client requirements.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Attention to detail</strong>: Ensure precision and accuracy in designs.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Empathy</strong>: Understand users' needs and perspectives to create designs that resonate with them.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Passion</strong>: Stay motivated to continuously improve and innovate.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Flexibility</strong>: Adapt to changes and embrace new technologies, trends, and methodologies.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Teamwork</strong>: Collaborate effectively with colleagues, clients, and stakeholders to achieve common design goals.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Analytical skills</strong>: Evaluate design solutions and make informed decisions</li></ul><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">How I’d become a designer if I was starting today</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">Before taking a UX course, I recommend first learning visual or graphic design. I say this because UX is far more complicated and requires many varied and different skills. Having that foundation of visual design definitely helps a UXer to communicate their design concepts.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">If you want to become a product designer, the easier path would be Visual Design —&gt; UX Design —&gt; Product Design, because product design requires technological, marketing, and strategic knowledge.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Because I had a product design degree before going into the workforce and learning visual design, I already had the foundational logic to be able to tackle UX design, which I see as quite a scientific pursuit.</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">The downsides of creative careers in tech</strong></h2><p id=""></p><p id="">The down-side of this career can be anytime you’re stuck with a toxic manager, colleagues, or clients.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">But this can happen in any career you choose. The antidote to this is to focus also on personal growth—things such as knowing when to say no, controlling clients’ pay schedules, calling out toxic situations, and sometimes just having the strength to walk away. That’s where an emergency fund becomes useful!</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">Creative tech career paths to explore</strong></h2><p id="">The right path for you is the one that you feel <strong id="">excited </strong>about.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Another way to approach this is to think back to when you were a child. I was always drawing and playing with Lego and asking “why” questions.</p><p id=""></p><p id="">I was OK academically, but was always way more of a visual communicator. Often, what you’re interested in and what you’re good at are the same things, purely because you spend time getting better at things you’re interested in.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><p id="">Before you really commit, try watching videos about what a day is like for that job. Reach out to people to ask questions. Join events!&nbsp;</p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re a visual person, explore UI Design</strong></h3><p id="">Crafting understandable visual interfaces that captivate and engage users.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re visual yet also a bit scientific and like data, explore UX Design&nbsp;</strong></h3><p id="">Shaping digital experiences that resonate with audiences.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re a mix of sociable and also scientific, explore UX Research / UX Testing</strong></h3><p id="">Conducting in-depth research and testing to refine digital products and ensure they meet user needs and preferences.</p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re quite visual and a jack of all trades, explore UX/UI Designer</strong></h3><p id="">Designing digital interfaces that not only prioritize user experience, but also ensure seamless interactions and visually appealing designs.&nbsp;</p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re a really organized person, explore Product Management&nbsp;</strong></h3><p id="">Overseeing the entire lifecycle from ideation to launch and beyond.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you’re good at logic and information architecture, you might try</strong></h3><h3 id=""><strong id="">Developer / Coding / Software Engineer</strong></h3><p id="">Building robust solutions that power the digital landscape.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you like analyzing things, finding patterns, making arguments or graphs, explore Data Science / Data Analysis</strong></h3><p id="">Unraveling insights that fuel informed decision-making and innovation in tech.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">If you like persuading people, explore Digital Marketing</strong></h3><p id="">Exploring the intersection of technology and marketing, leveraging data-driven strategies to drive engagement and growth.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">Final thoughts</strong></h3><p id="">I’d advise that if you want to be successful in a creative tech career, get ready for a considerable and continuous commitment to skilling up.&nbsp;</p><p id="">From the outside, having a career like mine probably looks like I know exactly what to do all the time; however, in reality, every day is about prioritizing, solving problems, designing experiences and visuals, and researching and measuring impact to the best of my ability, based on my past experience. There’s no set routine and I’m learning constantly.</p><p id="">If you want to have a creative tech career, some important qualities are: resilience, curiosity, and a passion for innovation. Embrace challenges as opportunities for growth, and aim high.&nbsp;</p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">Author bio</strong></h2><p id="">Rachael Page is an experienced Product Designer with extensive experience in the tech industry.
