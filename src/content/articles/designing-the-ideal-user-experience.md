<h3 id="">What is "Blue-sky thinking" exactly...</h3><p id="">also known as "outside-the-box thinking," it's a creative and unconstrained approach to brainstorming, problem-solving, or ideation. It involves generating innovative ideas and solutions <strong id="">without being limited by practical constraints, existing limitations, or traditional ways of thinking</strong>.</p><p id="">In blue-sky thinking, individuals are encouraged to think freely and expansively, imagining possibilities that might seem far-fetched or unconventional at first glance. The term "blue-sky" suggests a clear, open, and boundless space where ideas can soar without being hindered by barriers.</p><p id="">Key aspects of blue-sky thinking include:</p><p id="">1. **<strong id="">Creativity:</strong>** Blue-sky thinking encourages creative thinking that goes beyond conventional norms. It involves exploring new perspectives, questioning assumptions, and connecting seemingly unrelated concepts.</p><p id="">2. **<strong id="">No Constraints:</strong>** During blue-sky thinking, participants intentionally set aside practical constraints such as budget, feasibility, or existing limitations. This enables them to explore ideas without being restricted by real-world barriers.</p><p id="">3. **<strong id="">Innovation:</strong>** The primary aim of blue-sky thinking is to foster innovation by pushing the boundaries of what is currently known or accepted. It often leads to breakthrough ideas and concepts.</p><p id="">4. **<strong id="">Diverse Ideas</strong>:** Blue-sky thinking encourages participants to contribute a wide range of ideas, regardless of how unconventional or unlikely they may seem. This diversity of thought can lead to unexpected insights.</p><p id="">5. **<strong id="">Free Flow of Ideas</strong>:** Participants are encouraged to share their ideas openly and without fear of criticism. This creates an environment where even wild or seemingly impractical ideas can be voiced.</p><p id="">6. **<strong id="">Exploration of Possibilities</strong>:** Blue-sky thinking encourages participants to explore the full spectrum of possibilities, even those that might seem implausible at first glance. It's about generating a pool of ideas from which more practical solutions can later be refined.</p><p id="">7. **<strong id="">Idea Generation</strong>:** The goal is to generate a large quantity of ideas, as this increases the chances of finding unique and valuable concepts.</p><p id="">8. **<strong id="">Early Stages of Innovation</strong>:** Blue-sky thinking is often used in the early stages of innovation and ideation processes. Once a range of ideas has been generated, they can be evaluated, refined, and narrowed down to more viable options.</p><p id="">Overall, blue-sky thinking is a valuable tool for fostering creativity, breaking through mental barriers, and sparking innovative solutions. It encourages individuals and teams to approach problems with an open mind and explore uncharted territories in the pursuit of new ideas.</p><p id=""></p><h5 id=""><strong id=""><em id="">Blue-sky thinking isn't usually taught in UX Bootcamps, they only teach the UX process after a concept has already been chosen. This, along with foundational visual design principles is one of the major missing skills when creating a great UX mind. </em></strong></h5><p id=""></p><h3 id="">Take the example of a health app:</h3><p id="">As a tutor at Career Foundry, I watch as every student selects 1 of 4 concepts, one of which is a health app. Every student who works on the health app project without fail mentions that people are too busy and stressed - this is never challenged, not are the root causes. </p><p id="">It strikes me that stress and lack of time are either a problem of society or a problem of individual mindset, or both. It makes me wonder if there could be a way to attack the root causes rather than just the symptoms - and this could be a unique approach for a student.</p><p id=""></p><h4 id="">Attacking problems at their root to design an ideal life</h4><p id="">This also goes over into the mental health area. Rather than just accepting ones lot in life, an example of changing individual mindset is that finding remote work takes back around 3 hours every single day from cessation of the commute, which can then be spent on a 30-60 minute jog and some home weights for example. </p><p id="">This specific solution won't suit 100% of people - but when finding health improvements, the first step is to understand an individuals' current situation and find appropriate and attainable solutions which do improve health over time. </p><p id=""></p><h4 id="">Creating habits is important for health</h4><p id="">As an ex-personal trainer I have some insight to offer into health and fitness which may be useful. The great majority of people doing fitness training who are under 35 ish are only doing it to look better e.g. be thinner or more attractive. Very few understand the long term implications of really looking after their body e.g. such as avoiding strokes or Alzheimer's. </p><p id="">Anyone who hasn't got into a fitness regimen by the time they are 35 will most likely never do it. e.g. most baby boomers who are 65+ now are very sedentary and in places like Germany where I live, they eat meat and diary 3 times per day and are having stents put in all over themselves to prevent heart attacks (not kidding). </p><p id=""></p><h4 id="">Failure often comes from a lack of knowledge, motivation and vision to future goal achievement</h4><p id="">Secondly, the vast majority don't have the knowledge to create or motivate themselves to follow a health plan. So, when a student states:</p><p id="">"Enables users to create their own health plan, provides support and offers rewards program to keep users motivated, happier and healthier"</p><p id="">I would encourage careful consideration - because 'creating their own plan' isn't likely to happen (they would be able to choose goals and answer questions about current status though). The other thing is that people are motivated by different things, I for example am not at all interested in rewards and I don't react well to badges or gamifications. </p><p id="">I prefer seeing results over time on a graph which shows a trend*, or other things. You will find that there are groups of people who are motivated in different ways, and that is what your user research and personas are there to find and document. </p><p id="">*remember also though that on the other side of 40 or 50, some users may be declining and so trend graphs may not be motivational. It could be more motivational to show how far better they are than the average person of their type (gender, age, height)</p><p id=""></p><h4 id="">Don't force time-poor people to spend time entering data</h4><p id="">Most new students create UX which creates a lot of tasks for the users, when they should be instead setting everything up by default with only fine-tuning by the user as they wish.</p><p id="">People who are time poor will not spend time entering data into an app - the app needs to automatically collect data e.g. from exercising/sleeping/other things , and there are many items like smart watches which help with this now. You can utilise this in any design. I'm sure that in the future it will be possible to to take a photograph of a meal and have AI enter the approximate nutrition points into an app.</p><p id=""></p><h4 id="">Measure, act, measure, adjust plan, act - repeat</h4><p id="">The route to true health is a long term balanced approach for example where every day the body has enough water, sleep, nutrition, exercise to support itself in the longer term. Your body today is more affected by what you did yesterday, quite affected by what you did in the last week, but less affected by what you did 10 years ago - but it all has a cumulative effect. Conversely today is the most important time for health. Therefore it's important to concentrate on the 'now' when it comes to health, fitness and longevity. </p><p id="">Summing up, anyone creating a health app has an opportunity to do something simple enough, but that which breaks out of the mould of how everyone else in the industry has been approaching this subject. With some 'out of the box' thinking. </p><p id="">I recommend reading about human motivational types. e.g <a id="" href="https://www.verywellmind.com/what-is-motivation-2795378" target="_blank">here is a start</a>, and remember that there are many theories, some are likely bogus.</p><h4 id="">Turning bad habits into good ones</h4><p id="">When building habits, certain chemicals are released in the brain, these same chemicals are what keeps people addicted to social media. If the experience designer understands mental process such as these and can harness them for the purpose, the best chance at a very exciting project outcome results. </p><p id=""></p><h2 id="">This is what true UX is all about, not apps which have nice UI. </h2><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><sup id="">How AI can be used to generate UX ideas</sup></h3><h4 id=""><sup id="">Prompt: "How could dopamine and user flows be used in a fitness app to encourage regular health improvements?"</sup></h4><p id=""><sup id="">Dopamine and user flows can be utilized in a fitness app to create a rewarding and engaging experience that encourages regular health improvements. Dopamine is a neurotransmitter associated with motivation, reward, and pleasure, and by incorporating elements that trigger dopamine release in the brain, such as gamification and positive feedback, a fitness app can boost user motivation and adherence to healthy behaviors. User flows, or the sequences of actions users take within an app, can also be designed strategically to guide users towards regular health improvements. Here are some specific ways dopamine and user flows can be used in a fitness app:</sup></p><ol id=""><li id=""><sup id="">Gamification: Incorporate gamified elements, such as badges, points, levels, or challenges, that users can earn as they progress towards their fitness goals. This creates a sense of achievement and rewards users with dopamine releases, motivating them to continue using the app and making progress in their health journey.</sup></li><li id=""><sup id="">Positive Feedback: Provide positive feedback and reinforcement to users when they complete a workout, achieve a personal best, or reach a milestone. This can be in the form of encouraging messages, visual cues, or celebratory animations. Positive feedback triggers dopamine release, making users feel accomplished and motivated to continue their healthy behaviors.</sup></li><li id=""><sup id="">Social Interaction: Allow users to connect with friends or other users within the app to create a sense of community and competition. This can be through features such as leaderboards, challenges, or social sharing. Social interaction can trigger dopamine release through social reward and peer recognition, fostering a sense of belonging and motivation to improve health.</sup></li><li id=""><sup id="">Personalization: Use data from user inputs, such as their fitness level, goals, and preferences, to personalize the app's content and recommendations. This creates a sense of ownership and relevance for users, as the app feels tailored to their needs. When users see their progress and receive personalized recommendations, it can boost their motivation and engagement.</sup></li><li id=""><sup id="">Clear User Flows: Design clear and intuitive user flows within the app that guide users towards regular health improvements. This can include setting up personalized goals, tracking progress, providing feedback, and suggesting next steps. A well-designed user flow can create a seamless and engaging experience that keeps users motivated and on track.</sup></li><li id=""><sup id="">Micro-rewards: Incorporate small rewards or surprises along the user journey, such as unlocking new workout routines, accessing premium content, or receiving discounts on fitness gear. These micro-rewards can trigger dopamine release, providing instant gratification and motivation for users to continue using the app and making progress in their fitness journey.</sup></li></ol><p id=""><sup id="">By combining elements that trigger dopamine release, such as gamification, positive feedback, social interaction, personalization, and clear user flows, a fitness app can create an engaging and rewarding experience that encourages regular health improvements.
