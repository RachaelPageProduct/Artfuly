<p id=""><em id="">(Photo by </em><a id="" href="https://unsplash.com/@benkolde?utm_content=creditCopyText&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=unsplash"><em id="">Ben Kolde</em></a><em id=""> on </em><a id="" href="https://unsplash.com/photos/white-and-black-laptop-bs2Ba7t69mM?utm_content=creditCopyText&utm_medium=referral&utm_source=unsplash"><em id="">Unsplash)</em></a></p><p id="">When it's time to create or update your Product Design or UX Portfolio, you'll want to <strong id="">gauge the competition, collect ideas for structure, layout, visual design and things that really stand out</strong> to help you land your dream job..</p><p id=""></p><p id="">After helping many designers build job-ready portfolios, there are a few designers that I recommend for inspiration for various reasons</p><ol id="" start="1"><li id=""><strong id="">Great structure</strong>, both on the site structure and the case study pages. Good structure helps busy recruiters and hiring managers to quickly find the information they need. </li><li id=""><strong id="">Great communication</strong> - who they are and what they do has been made very clear, and/or they have mastered communicating the experience design that they have created</li><li id=""><strong id="">Standing out </strong>- use of animation or visual design, perhaps having a lot of 'character'. It's important to stand out if you want to beat the crowd and land the job - I'll call this '<strong id="">Bling</strong>'!</li></ol><p id=""></p><h3 id="">Remember to design the 'Experience' that the recruiter is having</h3><p id="">Many applicants forget to apply the UX principles to their job application. I often find links to portfolios with friction - e.g. the link takes the viewer to a place other than the folio homepage or the mentioned case study page.</p><p id="">Make sure that you step through the process that the recruiter is having, open your resume/CV and check if your portfolio link can be easily found? Does it jump out or is it hidden in a lump of text? Click that link - does is open up directly to your portfolio homepage?</p><p id="">Have you mentioned relevant case studies in your cover letter? Do the links actually work?</p><p id="">Have you named your Resume/CV file with your name and job title and date? e.g. "Rachael Page - Product Designer - Feb 2024" (or a similar way)? Remember that people will store your resume and you need to make it easier for them to find it. </p><p id="">Have you included a way for them to contact you and get an up-to-date version?</p><p id="">There's a lot more that could be said, but do apply your UX principles to the experience that the hiring person is having!!!</p><p id=""></p><p id=""><strong id=""><em id="">I've broken this list up into Website pages and case study pages and good and bad examples of each - because, whilst you can learn from the best, you can also learn what not to do from the worst...</em></strong></p><p id=""></p><h3 id="">Good Portfolio Websites &amp; Homepages</h3><h4 id=""><a id="" href="https://www.bryntaylor.co.uk/" target="_blank">Bryn Taylor</a></h4><p id="">Platform - Webflow</p><p id="">Type of folio - Product Design</p><figure id="" class="w-richtext-figure-type-image w-richtext-align-fullwidth" style="max-width:1820px" data-rt-type="image" data-rt-align="fullwidth" data-rt-max-width="1820px"><a id="" href="https://www.bryntaylor.co.uk/" target="_blank"><div id=""><img id="" alt="" src="https://uploads-ssl.webflow.com/5f6c7756eae6eb60a54c6c38/65c1fe90555c0f432e24cbc4_Screenshot%202024-02-06%20at%2010.40.19.png" width="auto" height="auto" loading="lazy"></div></a></figure><h5 id="">Why it's good</h5><p id="">Bryn has a sophisticated yet simple sense of visual design, the site is well structured and I think a recruiter would find this a joy to read since it's text minimal yet communicates quickly.
