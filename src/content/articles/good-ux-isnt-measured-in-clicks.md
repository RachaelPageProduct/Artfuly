<p id="">Good UX Isn’t Measured in as few clicks to the end goal, it's actually measured in real business metrics such as 'Increased Conversions' or 'Increased Sales per time period'. Here's why:</p><p id="">When evaluating user experience (UX), it's tempting to reduce its success to a single metric, such as the number of clicks it takes for a user to complete a task. However, this oversimplifies what good UX truly entails. UX is about guiding users through an experience that balances efficiency with persuasion, trust-building, and information delivery—not just a race to minimise clicks.</p><h3 id="">Why Fewer Clicks Isn’t Always Better</h3><p id="">While a streamlined user journey can be beneficial, some users need more time and information to make decisions. For instance, in e-commerce, the goal isn’t just to funnel users straight to checkout; it’s to help them feel confident in their purchase. Building trust, showcasing value, and dispelling fears might require multiple steps or pages, and that's perfectly okay.</p><p id="">For example, consider these key user questions that a well-designed journey should address:</p><ol id="" start="1"><li id=""><strong id="">WHAT is this?</strong><br>Users need to understand the product or offer immediately. This might involve an eye-catching headline, a clear description, or visuals that make the product’s value unmissable.</li><li id=""><strong id="">WHY is it valuable?</strong><br>Highlight features, benefits, and unique selling points. Customer testimonials, reviews, or case studies can reinforce trust and demonstrate the value of the product.</li><li id=""><strong id="">HOW does it work?</strong><br>For complex products or services, clear instructions or demonstrations—via videos, step-by-step guides, or FAQs—can help users visualise themselves using the product successfully.</li><li id=""><strong id="">WHEN is the right time?</strong><br>Creating urgency (e.g., limited-time offers) or answering practical concerns about delivery timelines or event schedules can nudge users towards action.</li><li id=""><strong id="">WHERE is it relevant?</strong><br>Geographic relevance or contextual use cases (e.g., “Perfect for your backyard” or “Runs locally in your city”) can help users envision the product in their lives.</li></ol><p id="">These questions don’t have to be addressed in a specific order, but each must be answered to meet user needs and build the confidence required for conversion.</p><h3 id="">The Role of Persuasive Design</h3><p id="">Good UX involves providing the right information at the right time. This means:</p><ul id=""><li id=""><strong id="">Creating dedicated pages</strong> for specific aspects of the user journey, such as a features page, testimonials page, or pricing breakdown.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Avoiding information overload</strong> by focusing each page on a unique, user-centreed objective.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Ensuring clear navigation</strong> so users can easily find answers to their questions when they’re ready.</li></ul><p id="">Each user's journey is different. Some may be ready to buy immediately, while others need time to explore and evaluate. The goal is to provide persuasive information—structured logically and seamlessly—while always maintaining a clear path to conversion.</p><h3 id="">Landing Pages and Conversion Funnels</h3><p id="">Landing pages, especially those tied to ads, play a critical role in user conversion. These pages should reflect the promise made in the ad to maintain continuity and build trust. For example, if an ad reads, “Automatically identify birds in your yard,” the landing page should prominently feature that same message as the headline. Breaking this thread creates confusion and can lead users to abandon the site.</p><p id="">Key tips for effective landing pages:</p><ul id=""><li id=""><strong id="">Strong thematic focus</strong>: Match the ad’s messaging and design.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Highlight immediate value</strong>: Clearly state the benefit users will gain.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Streamline distractions</strong>: Remove unnecessary links or information that might divert users from the intended action.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Call-to-action clarity</strong>: Ensure the desired next step (e.g., sign up, buy, learn more) is unmistakable and easy to act on.</li></ul><h3 id="">Conversion Funnels Are Not One-Size-Fits-All</h3><p id="">Every user is on a unique journey, influenced by their goals, concerns, and level of familiarity with your product or service. A well-structured funnel takes these differences into account. Key elements of a good conversion funnel include:</p><ul id=""><li id=""><strong id="">Top of Funnel (Awareness)</strong>: Provide educational content or attention-grabbing offers to engage new users.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Middle of Funnel (Consideration)</strong>: Offer detailed information and social proof to help users evaluate your product.</li><li id=""><strong id="">Bottom of Funnel (Decision)</strong>: Remove friction, emphasise urgency, and make the conversion process as simple as possible.</li></ul><h3 id="">Take a Scientific Approach</h3><p id="">Good UX isn’t about minimising clicks;<strong id=""> it’s about creating an experience that answers user needs, builds trust, and persuades effectively</strong>. Whether through a multi-step journey or a single landing page, the focus should always be on delivering <strong id="">the right information, at the right time, in the right way</strong>. In the end, success isn’t measured by the number of clicks, but by the quality of the experience and the outcomes it achieves.</p><p>Use tools like 'Amplitude' to measure one landing page against another, see definitively which one performs best, and see the reasons why. Sometimes a poorly performing landing page might overtake the other page with just one tweak. Continuous improvemment based on analytics is the right approach for any business who wants to be a winner. </p><p id=""></p>
