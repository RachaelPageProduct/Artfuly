<h2 id="">#1 Falling in love with your own idea</h2><p id=""><em id="">This is one of the biggest traps I see designers fall into:</em></p><p id="">The idea of artists and designers "falling in love with their own idea" refers to the common trap where someone becomes emotionally attached to their initial concept — so much so that they ignore evidence that it's not effective, not solving the problem, or not resonating with users. This is ego-driven attachment disguised as "vision."</p><p id=""></p><h3 id="">It often shows up as:</h3><p id="">Dismissing user feedback because “they don’t get it.”</p><p id="">Tweaking endlessly instead of pivoting.</p><p id="">Ignoring data that contradicts their assumptions.</p><p id="">Making things more about aesthetic or cleverness than function or clarity.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id="">How to Recognize It</h3><p id="">You feel defensive when someone critiques the idea.</p><p id="">You spend more time justifying it than improving it.</p><p id="">You're solving for your ego, not your audience.</p><p id="">You haven’t tested alternatives, but you’ve convinced yourself this is “the one.”</p><p id=""></p><h3 id="">How to Avoid It</h3><p id="">Validate early. Share work before you’re emotionally invested.</p><p id="">Test alternatives. Force yourself to try at least 2–3 other directions.</p><p id="">Detach from outcome. Care more about solving the problem than being right.</p><p id="">Revisit the brief. Ask: Is this actually serving the goal or just serving my taste?</p><p id="">Bring in outsiders. Feedback from people not emotionally invested is gold.</p><p id=""></p><p id=""><strong id="">Recognising how to avoid falling in love with your own solution or design, is what separates a pro from a stubborn amateur.</strong></p><p id=""></p><h2 id=""><strong id="">#2 Listening to user feedback about what they think other people might think</strong></h2><p id=""><strong id=""></strong><br><em id="">This is one of the sneakiest types of noise that shows up in user testing:</em></p><h3 id=""><br>Participants often make comments like:</h3><p id=""><br><em id="">“Older people wouldn’t be able to read this.”</em><br><em id="">“This colour scheme might not appeal to everyone.”</em><br><em id="">“Someone from another country might misunderstand this part.”</em></p><p id=""></p><p id="">They’re not actually giving you their own reaction — they’re guessing at what <em id="">other</em> people might think. This kind of secondhand feedback might feel insightful, but it’s unreliable. It’s speculation, not data.</p><p id="">People are terrible at predicting how <em id="">other</em> people will respond. And when you act on it, you risk solving fake problems.</p><p id=""></p><h3 id=""><strong id="">How to Recognize It</strong></h3><ul id=""><li id="">The sentence starts with “people might think...” or “some users may feel...”</li><li id="">They don’t express their <em id="">own</em> confusion or dislike, just imagined ones.</li><li id="">They seem confident about others’ views without evidence.</li><li id="">They haven’t actually struggled with the issue themselves.<br></li></ul><h3 id=""><strong id="">How to Handle It</strong></h3><ul id=""><li id="">Gently ask: <em id="">“How do you personally feel about it?”</em></li><li id="">Redirect: <em id="">“Did that confuse you?” or “Would you use it as it is?”</em></li><li id="">Don’t log it as a usability issue unless they show personal friction.</li><li id="">If the point comes up repeatedly across participants, investigate. But verify with real data.</li></ul><p id=""><strong id="">Getting distracted by proxy feedback is a fast way to dilute your product.</strong><br>Design for the person in front of you, not the ghosts they imagine.</p>
